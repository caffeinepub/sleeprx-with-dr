30-Day Hair Fall Reduction Guide

Evidence-based steps, nutrition protocols & daily habits — starting today.
How to use this guide: Follow the weekly phases in order. Each week builds on the last. You do not need to do everything perfectly — consistency matters more than perfection. Most people notice reduced shedding by Day 14–21.

Part 1 — Nutritional Foundation

Hair follicles are among the most metabolically active cells in the body. Deficiencies in key nutrients are one of the most common and overlooked causes of hair fall.

Part 2 — 30-Day Step-by-Step Protocol

Week 1 — Days 1–7
Foundation: Scalp Reset & Nutrition Audit
  • Day 1: Take a photo of your hairline and parting. This is your baseline.
  • Day 1–2: Review your diet using the table above. Identify your biggest nutritional gaps.
  • Day 2: Start taking a basic blood test (ferritin, Vitamin D, zinc) if you haven't in 6 months.
  • Day 3: Switch to a sulphate-free, gentle shampoo. Wash hair only 2–3x per week.
  • Day 3–7: Begin scalp massage daily for 4 minutes using fingertips (no oil required). This stimulates microcirculation.
  • Day 4: Eliminate or significantly reduce: crash dieting, refined sugar, alcohol, and seed oils.
  • Day 5–7: Begin eating the daily meal plan. Focus on iron + protein at every main meal.
  • Daily: Drink 2.5–3L of water. Dehydration affects scalp oil production and follicle health.
Week 2 — Days 8–14
Topical Treatment: Oil & Serum Routine
  • Day 8: Introduce a hair growth oil (rosemary, castor, or burdock-based). Apply 1–2x per week before washing.
  • Day 8–14: Continue daily scalp massage. Increase to 5–6 minutes.
  • Day 9: Add a rosemary spray or hair serum to your post-wash routine (apply to damp scalp).
  • Day 10: Avoid heat styling if possible. If needed, always use a heat protectant.
  • Day 11: Sleep on a silk or satin pillowcase to reduce overnight mechanical breakage.
  • Day 12: Check supplement gaps — start Vitamin D if deficient (with doctor guidance).
  • Day 13–14: Review your stress levels. High cortisol is a major driver of telogen effluvium (stress-related hair fall).
Week 3 — Days 15–21
Consistency & Stress Protocol
  • Day 15: Take a second photo. Compare with Day 1. Shedding should begin to slow for most people.
  • Day 15–21: Add a 10-minute walk or light exercise daily. Exercise improves scalp blood flow and reduces cortisol.
  • Day 16: Try a weekly deep conditioning or hair mask treatment (focus on lengths and ends).
  • Day 17: Practice 5–10 minutes of breathwork or meditation before bed. Chronic stress is one of the top 3 causes of hair fall.
  • Day 18: Reassess your washing routine — over-washing strips natural oils; under-washing clogs follicles. 2–3x/week is optimal.
  • Day 19–21: Audit your hair ties and accessories. Avoid tight elastics and styles that pull at the hairline (traction alopecia).
Week 4 — Days 22–30
Optimise & Build Long-Term Habits
  • Day 22–24: Fine-tune your nutrition — add collagen-supporting foods (bone broth, citrus, berries) to support keratin production.
  • Day 25: Review your results. If blood work is back, address deficiencies with targeted supplementation.
  • Day 26: Consider adding a dermatologist-approved growth serum (peptides, caffeine, or minoxidil if recommended by a doctor).
  • Day 27: Do a scalp check — is there any dryness, flaking, or oiliness? Adjust your shampoo accordingly.
  • Day 28: Build your maintenance routine: weekly oil treatment, daily massage, nutrition-first eating, low-stress habits.
  • Day 29: Final photo comparison. Most people see 20–40% reduction in shedding and visible scalp coverage improvement.
  • Day 30: Continue your routine. Hair growth cycles are 3–6 months. Consistency beyond 30 days is where transformation happens.

Part 4 — What to Avoid

  • Crash dieting or extreme calorie restriction — Sudden drops in calories trigger telogen effluvium (mass shedding) 2–3 months later.
  • Excess Vitamin A supplements — High-dose Vitamin A is a known cause of hair loss. Don't exceed 10,000 IU/day unless medically directed.
  • Daily heat styling — Flat irons and blow dryers above 180°C damage the hair shaft permanently.
  • Sulphate-heavy shampoos — Strips natural oils, causes scalp dryness and inflammation.
  • Tight hairstyles daily — Ponytails, tight braids, and extensions can cause traction alopecia, which is permanent if sustained.
  • Ignoring blood work — Ferritin, Vitamin D, and thyroid levels are the three most commonly missed root causes of hair fall.
  • Self-diagnosing alopecia — If hair fall is sudden, patchy, or accompanied by scalp changes, see a dermatologist.
Remember: Hair fall reduces before new growth becomes visible. Don't stop your routine just because you don't see new hairs yet — the follicle must first return to its growth phase, which takes 6–12 weeks after the trigger is removed.
